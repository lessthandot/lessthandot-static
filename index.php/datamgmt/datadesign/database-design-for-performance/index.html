Database Designing With Performance In Mind

by Ted Krueger (onpnt) on January 22, 2013 in category Data Modelling and Design Database Administration Microsoft SQL Server Microsoft SQL Server Admin. Article views: 21,140
Tweet Instapaper

Database design is the foundation on which we can build a successful initiative for maintenance and scalability.Â  Administrators have the ability to maintain indexing, statistics, integrity checks and so on, but if a database is designed extremely poorly, even the tasks of indexing or maintaining statistics accurately, becomes very difficult.Â  In some cases, the database is all but lost and a redesign initiative is needed, costly massive expenses.

When considering a database design, thinking of performance such as, how would this be indexed, in mind.Â  While maintaining that thought process, also keep in mind the type of design that is being created.Â  Historically, OLTP and OLAP are the high level designs to choose from.Â  OLTP being a highly transactional or insert/update/delete, database design while OLAP, retains a wide table concept and de-normalized approach due to highly readable and little changes made.

In order to ascertain which of these designs required, there are two simple questions to ask.

  1. Is this for an OLTP or OLAP installation?
  2. Do I normalize or de-normalize?

These two questions are truly one and the same.Â  The first, OLTP or OLAP, dictates the second.Â  In an OLTP situation, what weâll talk about today, normalization is going to effectively be critical to the performance we will have.Â  On the same side, OLAP designed database can suffer drastically from a deeply normalized design due to the movement from high write to high read events.Â  With the normalization being so critical, the first stage in that is to meet some level of normalization in an OLTP situation.Â  If situations do present themselves where normalization has become a performance problem due to read/write variations, de-normalization can be effective.

Normalization is the standard process of reducing redundancy in a database.Â  Although there are many levels of normalization, called normalized form, weâre going to focus on a simplistic level of normalization and a secondary level or 2NF for the example shown in this article.Â  In this state of normalization, we will still see a high benefit in performance enhancements.

Internal Structure Effects

Internally, these designs also affect storage.Â  Since the designs are similar and the tables are relatively small, you may thing normalization isnât needed.Â  Normalization relates to performance, though.Â  Some of the poor designs lead to inability to enhance performance with features available due to growth or scalability.Â  Take a simple tuning effort such as the disk subsystems.Â  A common standard is to locate files of different read and write needs on differently configured disks.Â  In another simplistic example, allocating tables to specific physical disk heads can have a major increase on overall performance.Â  Given the poor design in Figure 1, we have few options to effectively perform some of these methods to increase performance For example, if we rarely update customer addresses but insert a high number of order headers, we would want order headers to be located on a highly writable disk configuration, with the address data on cheaper, slower disk.
